# CNVRT specialist SEO page competitor audit

**Research date:** 16 July 2026  
**Market:** United Kingdom, with additional attention to Manchester competitors  
**Pages in scope:** Main SEO hub, Local SEO, Technical SEO and Ecommerce SEO

## Decision

CNVRT should retain `/services/seo/` as the broad SEO service hub and publish only three specialist commercial pages:

1. `/services/local-seo/`
2. `/services/technical-seo/`
3. `/services/ecommerce-seo/`

These pages cover materially different buyer problems, delivery methods and measures of value. They should not become lightly rewritten versions of the main SEO page. Additional SEO subservice pages are not justified at this stage.

## Method

Ten prominent UK competitors were selected for their visibility, established search offering, specialist positioning, UK market relevance and useful coverage of at least one page in scope. This is a strategic competitor set, not a claim that an objective league table exists.

The audit reviewed live service pages for:

- Primary search intent and headline positioning
- Commercial framing and target buyer
- Service depth and technical accuracy
- Evidence, case studies and trust signals
- Calls to action and enquiry path
- Page structure, readability and repetition
- Internal linking and service relationships
- Gaps CNVRT can credibly use

Claims about how Google Search works were checked against current first-party Google documentation rather than competitor copy.

## Executive findings

The strongest competitors combine exact-intent headings with commercial outcomes, recognisable proof and a detailed explanation of delivery. The weakest pages confuse length with usefulness. Repeated definitions, inflated superlatives, generic statistics and unprioritised lists are common.

CNVRT can compete by being more specific about what changes, who implements it and how the work connects to enquiries or revenue. Its clearest differentiator is the ability to connect SEO strategy with web design, Shopify and development. This matters because many competitors diagnose technical problems but depend entirely on a separate development team to implement recommendations.

The content should be substantial, but it should earn its length. Every section needs to answer a buyer question, demonstrate understanding or move the visitor towards a credible next step.

## Ten-agency audit

### 1. Impression

**Pages reviewed:** [Technical SEO Agency](https://www.impressiondigital.com/seo/technical-seo/) and [Ecommerce SEO Agency](https://www.impressiondigital.com/blog/7-merchandising-questions-you-need-to-answer-before-black-friday/)

**Positioning:** Technical and ecommerce SEO tied explicitly to measurable business growth, conversions and revenue.

**What works:**

- Opens with the commercial reason for technical work instead of a dictionary definition.
- Supports claims with recognisable case studies, client quotes and awards.
- Explains multiple engagement models, including one-off audits, migration support and ongoing consultancy.
- Covers implementation reality through ticket creation and liaison with developers.
- Connects performance, crawlability and user experience rather than treating them as separate concerns.
- Ecommerce copy segments experience by retail category and keeps transactions central.

**Weaknesses and openings:**

- The tone and proof skew towards larger enterprise clients.
- The volume of awards, testimonials and service variants creates a long decision path.
- Local SEO is not a clearly differentiated core landing page in the reviewed UK structure.
- CNVRT can go further by stating that development can be delivered, not only briefed to another team.

### 2. SALT.agency

**Pages reviewed:** [Technical SEO Services](https://salt.agency/expertise/technical-seo/) and [Ecommerce SEO Services](https://salt.agency/expertise/ecommerce-seo/)

**Positioning:** Deep enterprise technical expertise, modern search visibility and commercial SEO for complex organisations.

**What works:**

- Establishes a specialist technical identity immediately.
- Explains technical debt and the cumulative cost of structural problems well.
- Names platforms and complex environments, which helps buyers self-qualify.
- Ecommerce coverage includes category optimisation, structured data, Core Web Vitals, international SEO and Merchant Center.
- Uses testimonials that discuss practical recommendations and measurable performance.
- Introduces revenue operations language to move discussion beyond traffic.

**Weaknesses and openings:**

- The specialist language can feel inaccessible to smaller and mid-market buyers.
- Some AI-search framing is repeated across the technical copy.
- The service is positioned as consultancy, so implementation ownership can remain unclear.
- Local SEO is not a central service page in the reviewed set.
- CNVRT can offer a clearer path from issue to fix to validated release.

### 3. Re:signal

**Page reviewed:** [Global Ecommerce SEO Agency](https://resignal.com/)

**Positioning:** Accountable organic growth for ecommerce brands, forecast in revenue rather than ranking movement.

**What works:**

- The opening proposition is unusually commercial and concise.
- Revenue forecasting, experimentation and customer search behaviour create a distinct methodology.
- Strong ecommerce case-study figures make the offer credible for larger retailers.
- Covers traditional search, retail search, AI search and social discovery within one ecommerce system.
- Repeats the metric that matters, revenue, consistently.

**Weaknesses and openings:**

- The positioning is designed for established global ecommerce brands and may exclude ambitious smaller retailers.
- Detailed delivery mechanics are less visible than the strategic promise on the main page.
- Local and general technical SEO buyers are not served by the main positioning.
- CNVRT cannot match the scale of the case-study roster yet, but can be more concrete about Shopify architecture, development and implementation.

### 4. Blue Array

**Page reviewed:** [Technical SEO Services](https://www.bluearray.co.uk/services/technical-seo/)

**Positioning:** Precise, prioritised technical SEO support that complements an internal team.

**What works:**

- Explains crawl, render and index problems in clear commercial language.
- Gives buyers visible service packages and levels of access.
- Strong testimonial connects technical work with business goals and migration support.
- Communicates flexibility across consultancy, projects and retained support.
- Keeps the page relatively focused compared with several competitors.

**Weaknesses and openings:**

- Day-based packages can make the buyer compare capacity rather than outcomes.
- The page is less explicit about who implements fixes.
- Ecommerce and local specialisms are not developed on the reviewed technical page.
- CNVRT should avoid package tables until its scope and recurring commercial model are confirmed.

### 5. The SEO Works

**Pages reviewed:** [Technical SEO Agency](https://www.seoworks.co.uk/seo-services/technical-seo-agency/), [Local SEO Services](https://www.seoworks.co.uk/local-seo/) and [Ecommerce SEO Services](https://www.seoworks.co.uk/ecommerce-website-seo/)

**Positioning:** Award-winning, full-service SEO designed to generate customers, enquiries and profitable ecommerce growth.

**What works:**

- Owns exact commercial search terms with direct titles and H1s.
- Has the broadest complete set of the three specialist pages audited.
- Uses a free audit as a low-friction conversion route.
- Local SEO includes Business Profile work, citations, local pages, schema and tracked calls or enquiries.
- Ecommerce SEO covers priorities by margin and seasonality, platforms, category and product optimisation, internal linking and organic revenue reporting.
- Technical SEO covers crawl, render, index, architecture, performance, structured data and platform-specific constraints.
- FAQs answer cost, timing, suitability, platforms and measurement questions.

**Weaknesses and openings:**

- Pages are very long and frequently repeat the same promise.
- Generic claims such as leading, best and award-winning dominate some sections.
- Several statistics are presented without visible first-party sourcing or current context.
- The volume of awards and repeated CTAs can feel aggressive.
- CNVRT should match the intent coverage but use fewer, sharper sections and more transparent limitations.

## Cross-market patterns

### What the strongest pages do

- Use the exact service category in the title, H1 and opening copy.
- Explain the business consequence before listing technical tasks.
- Show which kinds of company, platform or problem the service suits.
- Provide concrete coverage rather than saying the approach is bespoke and stopping there.
- Include case studies, testimonials, awards or named client logos near key decisions.
- Explain process, reporting, timing and common limitations.
- Use relevant FAQs to cover commercial objections and long-tail intent.
- Link specialist work back to wider SEO, content, development and paid acquisition.

### What weakens competitor pages

- Repeating the definition of the service in several sections.
- Using long unsourced statistics as a substitute for proprietary proof.
- Claiming to be leading, best or award-winning without explaining how work is different.
- Treating every technical checklist item as equally important.
- Promising rankings or implying control over results that search engines determine.
- Ending with a document or recommendation list without clarifying implementation.
- Publishing regional pages that only swap place names.
- Adding AI terminology to every section without changing the actual service.

## Current search guidance that must shape the copy

The pages should follow first-party guidance rather than competitor folklore:

- Google says local results are mainly based on relevance, distance and prominence. A service can improve relevance and prominence signals, but it cannot control the searcher’s distance or buy a better local ranking. See [Google Business Profile local ranking guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091?hl=en).
- Google’s ecommerce guidance covers product data, structured data, URL design, site structure, pagination, incremental loading and launch planning. See [Google Search Central ecommerce best practices](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/specialty/ecommerce).
- Product structured data can make price, availability, ratings, shipping and returns eligible for richer presentation, but rich results are not guaranteed. See [Product structured data guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/product).
- Technical SEO must consider crawlability, indexation, canonicals, sitemaps, robots controls, JavaScript rendering and site moves. See [Google crawling and indexing guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing).
- JavaScript sites pass through crawling, rendering and indexing, and server-side or pre-rendered HTML remains useful for users and crawlers. See [Google JavaScript SEO basics](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/javascript/javascript-seo-basics).
- Search Console supplies crawl, indexation, query, page and Core Web Vitals information, while Analytics helps connect landing-page activity to business behaviour. See [Google Search Console guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/monitor-debug/search-console-start).
- Google recommends substantial, original, people-first content and explicitly says there is no preferred word count. See [Google’s helpful content guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content).

## Recommended content architecture

### Main SEO hub

**Role:** Explain CNVRT’s complete organic-growth approach and route visitors into the right specialist workstream.

**Essential sections:**

1. SEO agency Manchester hero focused on qualified demand.
2. Commercial problems caused by weak technical foundations, unclear page ownership and content without purpose.
3. Outcomes covering demand, foundations, authority and measurement.
4. Scope covering strategy, content, on-page work, authority and reporting without reproducing specialist detail.
5. Heatons and Hear Better proof with evidence caveats respected.
6. Process from diagnosis to implementation and learning.
7. Three related cards linking Local, Technical and Ecommerce SEO.
8. FAQs on timing, audits, content, local support, reporting and website projects.

### Local SEO

**Primary buyer:** A location-based service business, retailer, clinic or multi-location organisation that needs more qualified local calls, directions, bookings and enquiries.

**Distinctive content:**

- Google Business Profile ownership, accuracy, categories, services, hours, media and landing-page relationships.
- Local keyword and service-area research based on real demand.
- Genuine location and service pages, not mass-produced place-name swaps.
- Consistent business details and useful citations.
- Review-generation process and response guidance, without fake or incentivised review tactics.
- Local links, mentions and authority opportunities.
- Multi-location governance where relevant.
- Measurement across Search Console, Business Profile performance, calls, forms, bookings and direction requests where tracking is available.
- Honest explanation that distance is not controllable and rankings are not guaranteed.

**Best conversion route:** Request a local visibility audit, with a secondary link to the Hear Better case study.

### Technical SEO

**Primary buyer:** A business with declining visibility, indexation issues, a complex site, a planned migration or an SEO programme that is not translating into results.

**Distinctive content:**

- Crawlability, rendering and indexation diagnosis.
- Robots directives, XML sitemaps, status codes, redirect chains and canonicals.
- Site architecture, internal linking and orphan-page discovery.
- JavaScript and headless rendering checks where relevant.
- Core Web Vitals and practical performance improvements.
- Structured data aligned with visible content.
- Migration, release and post-launch validation.
- Prioritised tickets with impact, effort, owner and acceptance criteria.
- Implementation by CNVRT where access and scope allow, or collaboration with the client’s developers.
- Monitoring through Search Console, analytics, crawls and release checks.

**Best conversion route:** Request a technical SEO audit, with a secondary route to Custom Web Development.

### Ecommerce SEO

**Primary buyer:** An online retailer that needs more non-brand discovery, stronger category visibility and organic revenue from a catalogue that can be complex to crawl and merchandise.

**Distinctive content:**

- Search-led category, collection and product architecture.
- Faceted navigation, parameters, variants, discontinued products and duplicate URL control.
- Product, category and buying-guide content shaped around intent and merchandising priorities.
- Product and merchant-listing structured data aligned with price, availability, shipping, returns and variants.
- Internal linking through navigation, collections, related products and editorial content.
- Product feed and Merchant Center considerations where included in scope.
- Core Web Vitals and mobile shopping performance.
- SEO migration support for Shopify and other replatforming projects.
- Measurement using non-brand visibility, qualified landing pages, assisted conversions and organic revenue.
- Heatons as primary proof, with UberKinky as migration and catalogue-supporting evidence.

**Best conversion route:** Request an ecommerce SEO review, with secondary links to Shopify and Shopify Migrations.

## Conversion requirements across all four pages

- Put the service and intended outcome in the title, H1 and first two paragraphs.
- Use one primary CTA above the fold and one lower-friction secondary route.
- Let the buyer recognise their situation before presenting a service list.
- Keep outcome cards equal in length and no taller than the established Web Design pattern.
- Use six capability cards with parallel depth and comparable line length.
- Include real proof, but do not repeat one case study in every paragraph.
- Explain implementation ownership and access requirements.
- State what cannot be guaranteed.
- Use FAQs for genuine buying questions, not repeated sales copy.
- Link to related services only where the next constraint is credible.
- Keep calls to action consistent: submit a project, request an audit or book a call.

## CNVRT strengths compared with the competitor set

1. **Design, development and SEO can be delivered together.** Many specialist agencies advise developers. CNVRT can implement technical, structural and conversion changes when the project provides suitable access and scope.
2. **Shopify and migration experience creates ecommerce credibility.** Heatons, UberKinky and Ya Omri give the Ecommerce SEO page a relevant commercial context beyond generic keyword optimisation.
3. **Local Manchester context is genuine.** CNVRT can target Manchester without manufacturing a network of location pages.
4. **The service system is visually clearer.** The existing editorial template can make substantial content easier to scan than the longest competitor pages.
5. **Connected services create practical next steps.** A technical constraint can move into development, an ecommerce constraint into Shopify, and a local discovery constraint into web or paid acquisition where appropriate.

## CNVRT weaknesses and controls

1. **The public SEO evidence set is still limited.** Keep unverified figures out of generic claims and complete evidence records for Heatons and Hear Better.
2. **The specialist team depth is not comparable with large agencies.** Do not imitate enterprise language or imply an 80-person specialist department. Emphasise founder-led ownership and connected delivery.
3. **Local SEO process evidence needs documenting.** The page can describe a responsible repeatable method, but Business Profile and local outcome claims need client approval before publication.
4. **Ecommerce SEO proof is adjacent rather than fully isolated.** Describe the SEO work within broader Shopify and growth engagements accurately. Do not claim every revenue result was caused by SEO alone.
5. **Authority-building delivery needs a precise boundary.** Avoid promising link volume or guaranteed placements. Frame authority work around relevant opportunities, digital PR collaboration and evidence the business can genuinely earn.

## Final recommendation

Publish the three specialist pages now, but treat their launch as the start of the evidence programme rather than the end. Their immediate advantage should come from accurate intent ownership, useful detail, honest limits, integrated implementation and better internal routes. Future gains should come from approved case-study evidence and a small number of strong decision-stage guides, not from adding more thin service pages.
